Rik's story

iHeel® exists because one student got tired of heel pads that quit after two days. This is the full story: the problem, the design, and the person behind it.

It started with a blister

In 2022 I was a student in the Netherlands, walking and cycling everywhere like everyone else here. New shoes meant one thing: that slow burn at the back of my heel by the end of the first day.

So I did what anyone would do. I bought heel pads. Then I bought different ones. Then more expensive ones. Every single pair peeled loose or fell apart within days. At some point I stopped blaming my shoes and started asking a better question: why do all heel pads fail the same way?

Why every pad I bought failed

The answer turned out to be hiding in how pads are made, not what they are made of. A heel pad is layers: cushioning foam and an adhesive backing, fused together at the edges. That fused edge is what holds the whole thing together.

Mass producers cut as many pads as possible per press with a single blade. Squeezing out those extra pads means cutting straight through the fused edges. The layers have nothing holding them together anymore, so the pad starts separating the moment your heel starts rubbing. That is why around 80% of pads on the market fail within days. It is not bad luck. It is the process.

“Every heel pad I bought failed within days. So I re-engineered the whole process myself.”

How iHeel® is designed

I decided the process was the product. Four decisions define every iHeel® pad:

Fewer pads per press

Small production batches with generous spacing, so every pad keeps its full fused edge. Fewer pads per press costs more per pad. It is the whole point.

Precision-cut edges

The cut runs outside the fusing line, never through it. The layers stay bonded for the life of the pad, which is measured in months, not days.

Materials that earn their place

Premium EVA cushioning foam that softens friction without collapsing, and a medical-grade self-adhesive backing that grips the shoe lining, not your skin.

Tested by hand, every batch

Every batch is checked before it ships. If I wouldn't wear it, I won't sell it. The rejection rate is under 1%, against an industry average of around 80%.

From Rik himself

People sometimes ask if there is a grand story behind iHeel®. There isn't. There is a student room, a pile of failed heel pads on a desk, and a stubborn streak I probably got from my parents.

I remember the exact evening it clicked. I had peeled yet another loose pad out of my shoe and instead of throwing it away, I pulled it apart to see how it was built. The layers came apart far too easily. That little autopsy became weeks of reading about die-cutting, calling suppliers who didn't take a student seriously, and testing prototypes on my own feet on the walk to lectures.

The photo next to this? That is me, around the time it all started. The hair is not real, the blisters after that party very much were. Walking home that night on sore heels, in shoes I had borrowed, I remember thinking: this is a solvable problem. Nobody has just bothered to solve it properly.

So I bothered. Five thousand customers later, I still test every batch myself, and I still answer the support inbox personally. Your pain was my pain too. That has been the whole business plan from day one.

Rik, founder of iHeel®
